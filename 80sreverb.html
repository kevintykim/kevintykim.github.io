Project Overview
Design a mobile app that allows users to listen to music from the 1980s.


Role
UI Designer

Tools
Adobe XD

Duration
1 month



Competitive Analysis

My first task was to analyze existing music apps, for two purposes: studying proven UI designs and identifying common user needs.

Search functions enable users to find the music they want.

Dashboard pages provide a general overview of the app’s content.
Navbars give users an easy, organized way to go deeper and find specific content.

Playlists add convenience by allowing users to quickly go to their favorite songs.



User Flow & Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Now with a better idea of how to flesh things out, I created my first draft of the app, compiling a tentative list of screens and arranging it into a user flow. This draft was rough, but it was a good base on which to continue the design process and expand my ideas later.

As I visualized the user flow on paper, my learnings from the earlier competitive analysis came in handy, providing inspiration for the screens’ layout templates.



Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

I used the low-fidelity sketches as a starting point, but I went in knowing changes would have to be made. This phase involved a good deal of experimentation with elements’ size, placement, and spacing. I also kept my mind open to ideating more screens (i.e., Welcome and Settings) and learning new ways to use the software, such as grid layouts.

The app took shape through these wireframes, but next came the task of bringing them to life with a distinct visual identity.



Icon Set

I brainstormed at least three potential icons for each function. The biggest challenge was choosing which icons would go well together into a consistently styled set. After some modifications, I decided on a group typified by 2-pixel wide lines and/or gaps (circled below).

I also considered how well the icons represented their functions, and gauged the risk of user confusion. For example, I rejected some Settings icons because they might be mistaken for suns instead of gears.



Mood Board

My goal was to create a mood that evoked the 1980s, so the app would transport users to the decade not just musically, but visually. This required extensive research into the ‘80s—particularly the nightlife and techno scene—to curate the proper mood elements and capture the retro aesthetic. Listening to ‘80s music during my research also helped me approach the task with the right mentality!



High-Fidelity Mockups

Before working on the final mockups, I looked back on the earlier phases with fresh eyes, in order to see what needed to be changed or improved. This included updating the user flow with more features (e.g., a search filter), expanding on the mood board’s imagery to include artists and album covers, and modifying the logo’s vectors to make the font look more rounded and inviting.

The final design phase was about refining and streamlining my rough draft work, and bringing it all together into a visually engaging music app that went above and beyond what I had first envisioned.



Retrospective

‘80s Reverb was created both out of interest in creating a music player app, and out of my own love for 1980s music. My aim was not merely to imitate the apps from my competitive analysis, but to build a stylish UI that reflected the spirit of the decade. I came away proud of my designs and features, but also eager to experiment with UI styles in the future. If I ever revisit the project or design a similar app, I would change aspects such as colors, icons, and grid layouts, to see if I can take different approaches to achieve the same overall tone.

This was also my first time using Adobe XD, and I took the opportunity to grow my skills with the software. Some parts of the design process were more engrossing than others—for example, the icon set provided a surprisingly enjoyable creative outlet, while the mood board required greater thought and concentration. However, I overcame any challenges by stepping back to engage with the material more slowly and patiently. In the end, ‘80s Reverb was valuable both as a learning experience and as a design project.

My biggest takeaway insights:
• When making low- OR mid-fidelity wireframes, don’t fall in love with your ideas. Always leave things open to change.
• Color and imagery should not be taken lightly. Carefully consider how potential users will receive your visuals.
• Take colorblind users into consideration. Vary the palette enough so that anybody can see the intended contrast.
• Design icons consistently. Pay close attention to details like angles and line weights, and apply similar stylings across the board.
• Sans serif fonts are good for body text, while more decorative and extravagant fonts should be saved for branding or large text.
