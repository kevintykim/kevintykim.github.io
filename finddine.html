Project Overview
Design a restaurant locator app, with four responsive screen widths.


Role
UI/UX Designer

Tools
Adobe XD

Duration
3 months



User Research

To get an understanding of how to make FindDine appealing, I interviewed potential users for their thoughts on existing food finder apps. Their answers yielded ideas for what to implement (search filters, detailed restaurant info) and what to avoid (feature clutter).



Minimum Viable Product

I boiled my user research findings down to a concrete vision: an app that lets users find restaurants quickly, and also lets them access detailed restaurant information just as quickly.




Exploratory Sketches

I created a simple design, in keeping with Hick’s law. The intent was to hide all of the detailed information by default, allowing users to understand the UI easily. The app would also feature intuitive buttons (represented by the three squares on the Restaurant Profile screen) to let users quickly jump to the detailed info they wanted.



Usability Test Results

To weed out pain points in the early sketches, I conducted a rapid prototype test with four participants. The test results revealed potential confusion with the UI elements, and also suggested that, contrary to my earlier thoughts, it would be better to show a limited amount of detailed info rather than hide all of it by default.

I was torn between two possible solutions for the first issue, and decided to conduct an A/B preference test to see whether I should use text or icons. But first I created more detailed wireframes, so the preference test could more accurately represent the final app screens.



Wireframes

In addition to implementing the usability test feedback, I took this opportunity to start working on the other breakpoints.



Preference Test Results

The vast majority of the testers found text more appealing than icons. Their rationales stressed how much more clarity this brought to the app, and informed my designs during the final mockup process.

Retrospective

As my first hands-on venture into the UX phase of design, FindDine was a valuable insight into processes such as user research and MVP hypothesis. One of my main goals with this app was to avoid an overcomplicated user interface, and my UX learnings helped me create a simple and efficient user journey.

Designing for a variety of screen widths was a great way for me to gain more experience with design software. The process of expanding my initial wireframes greatly improved my grasp of tools such as grid layouts and responsive resizing.

The project’s biggest roadblocks occurred during the mid-fidelity wireframing phase, where I created so many potential designs (e.g., text vs. icons, proper color use) that I became unsure which ideas to choose. To overcome this, I slowed down to focus and refresh myself, and even sought out online inspirations to see my fellow designers’ approaches to similar apps. The A/B preference test also allowed me to gauge user response to my work so far.

My most meaningful and impactful UX insights underlined the problem of apps presenting an overwhelming amount of information and obstructing users from their goals. People don’t want app interaction to feel like a struggle, especially when they’re hungry and in search of a good restaurant. This sentiment, above all others, wielded the most influence on FindDine’s creation.
